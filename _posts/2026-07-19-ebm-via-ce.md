With the recent push of World Models and the idea of AI systems that can understand the underlying physics of its environment and its behaviour with it led me to explore the idea of Energy-Based Models, in [the video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=A7AnCvYDQrU) was an eye opener to training visual self supervised models, within the two methods of training the models: contrastive and architectural methods, I experimented with contrastive methods and instead of using the standard Contrastive Divergence algorithm (CD-k) I have opted instead for the Noise-Contrastive Estimation method which utilises Categorical Cross Entropy like in Word2Vec and InfoNCE (has a lot of subsets under it). The full experiment is in this [Colab notebook](https://colab.research.google.com/drive/1ZeGIqHp1kZWzgN2qRbIrnr7FhUQoAVXX?usp=sharing).

The core problem initially is that standard regression is limited for EBMs such as the Mean Squared Error, which are not optimal for systems with multiple outcomes (this is why in the notebook I utilise a sine function to test the EBM) as it averages it out into something unphysical. So instead the solution was to structure the system into a critic of sorts such that when given both the input and output unlike a standard regression model which only predicts, thus system can evaluate structural harmony and rate ie- (E(x,y)), but rather than using the standard MCMC sampling for contrastive divergence I utilised a modern NCE and by framing training as a multi-class game against 31 uniform random noise fakes. By using the temperature scaled energies as logits ($-\frac{E}{\tau}$) , I was able to pull the energy of the true data down and the false data up.

The beautiful thing about this system is that it really relates to system 2 thinking, where in there is no longer a forward pass dictating the output rather it is an iterative process where the right answer has to be found via constant traversal through the energy terrain. We cant then use this rating sort of system by forcing it to predict freezing the input x and adding random noise, letting the model "think". In my initial run I used an SGD optimiser which led to an explosion of gradients which I quickly resolved by resorting instead to an Adam optimiser, the running variance calculation acted as a brake on steep slopes preventing overshooting and smooth particle motion. The final animation and 2D map provide empirical proof of convergence and hence the target infinity shape.

The underlying beauty of the math of the cross entropy function was obscured by torch.nn.functional.cross_entropy so instead here it is in all its glory -

$$L = -\log \left( \frac{e^{-E(x, y_{\text{pos}}) / \tau}}{e^{-E(x, y_{\text{pos}}) / \tau} + \sum_{i=1}^{31} e^{-E(x, y_{\text{fake}, i}) / \tau}} \right)$$

Where:$E(x, y_{\text{pos}})$: The energy score the MLP assigns to the true physical coordinate pair.$E(x, y_{\text{fake}, i})$: The energy score the MLP assigns to the $i$-th random fake coordinate pair in the lineup.$\tau$ (Temperature = 0.1): The scaling factor that amplifies the differences between the energy scores.

Breaking it down you can see how this forces the model to learn the figure-8 shape, we can split the equation into two parts using standard logarithm rules ($-\log(A/B) = \log(B) - \log(A)$):

$$L = \frac{E(x, y_{\text{pos}})}{\tau} + \log \left( e^{-E(x, y_{\text{pos}}) / \tau} + \sum_{i=1}^{31} e^{-E(x, y_{\text{fake}, i}) / \tau} \right)$$

When the Adam optimizer minimizes this loss over the entire batch, it is forced to do two things simultaneously :Minimize the Left Term ($\downarrow E(x, y_{\text{pos}})$): It aggressively drives the energy of the true physical data points down as low as possible. This is what scoops out the smooth, yellow, low-energy valley floor where $y = \pm\sin(x)$.Maximize the Right Term ($\uparrow E(x, y_{\text{fake}})$): It pushes the energy of the 31 random unphysical fakes up as high as possible. This is what builds the steep, towering purple walls of high energy out in the empty zones where no real data ever shows up.

To conclude, EBM's are incredibly capable of being prediction machines and are currently an incredible method of self supervised learning hence why it is used within the JEPA models by Yann LeCun, the only limitation with such models is the compute necessary for inference as instead of a single forward pass an iterative process is used which can be slow and intensive. Overall the future of the world of physical AI models seem very promising.
